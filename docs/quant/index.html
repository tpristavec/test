Quantitative

I look for answers to social science questions using advanced quantitative methods. I have a strong interest in quantitative analysis and am eager to develop competency in new techniques, programming languages, and to further my knowledge of workflow, collaboration, and reproducible research tools.

Training

I am proficient in R, Stata, Mplus, Git, bash, and LaTeX.

During my graduate training, I used primarily large, nationally representative datasets in my research work. I am thoroughly familiar with National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), National Health and Aging Trends Study (NHATS) data, National Study of Caregiving (NSOC) data, and Fragile Families and Child Wellbeing Study (FFCWS) data. As a research assistant, I also participated in collecting data and creating datasets for community-based research. At SDAD, I work with all kinds of data–from text corpora to web-scraped content and shapefiles.

I’ve taken courses in Sociological Research Methods (501), Statistical Methods in Sociology I (541), and Statistical Methods in Sociology II (542) at the Sociology Department at Rutgers’ New Brunswick campus. I then completed additional courses in statistics at the Rutgers Newark School of Criminal Justice, including Intermediate Statistics (543) and Advanced Statistics (641). These courses gave me a solid foundation in multiple regression, covering topics from ordinary least squares models and model diagnostics to models for cross-sectional outcomes that do not conform to a normal distribution (binary, ordered, multinomial, event count, and censored outcomes), maximum likelihood estimation, and models for data with nested or multilevel structure. Besides having in-depth knowledge of statistical analyses in a traditional regression setting, I also have extensive experience in conducting latent class analysis.

I’ve also completed additional methods training beyond my University.

In 2016, I participated in the ICPSR Summer Program in Quantitative Methods of Social Research as a Clifford C. Clogg Scholar. I took month-long courses in Longitudinal Analysis and Structural Equation Models with Latent Variables, attended the four-week Blalock Lectures in Advanced Topics in Social Research, two-week Introduction to the R Statistical Computing Environment and Review Lectures on Matrix Algebra, and the one-time Introduction to the LaTeX Text Processing System seminar. In addition to formal training, I am always furthering my knowledge in new techniques, programming languages, and workflow tools through self-paced online courses.

In 2017/18, I participated in Rutgers Digital Humanities Initiative workshops on Thematic Maps in QGIS, Collecting Social Media Data for Research, and Manipulating and Analyzing Social Media Data in R. I extended my social media analytics and data science knowledge at the 8th Annual Arizona Methods Workshop, where I completed the Data Science in R course (focusing on machine learning and social media data) and received an introduction to sequence analysis. In additon, I am currently completing the Machine Learning A-Z: Hands-On Python & R In Data Science Udemy online course.

Teaching

In 2016/17, I gave invited lectures on Introduction to Stata and Categorical Independent Variables for Rutgers Sociology Department’s graduate course Statistical Methods in Sociology I and II. I gave a lecture on Cross-Tabulations using the General Social Survey Website for the graduate course Social Research Methods. I also led an Introduction to R workshop for the department’s Quantitative Applications Workshop. As a graduate lab and statistics teaching assistant, I advised early-stage graduate students on research projects, and held statistical consultation hours open all faculty, graduate, and undergraduate members of my department.
